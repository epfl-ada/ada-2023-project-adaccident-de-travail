Release Analysis

Examining the release dates of movies provided valuable insights into temporal trends as it sheds light the evolving dynamics within the film industry.

Release Year Trends

Nowadays, spectactors have the feeling of being submerge with sequels after sequels of their favorite movies. But does this trend really shows up in data? We analyzed the release date of more than 80'0000 films from 1888 to 2016

Indeed, our analysis on the release years reveals a shifting landscape in the production and distribution of movies. Over time, there is an increase in the overall number of movies hitting the screens. Moreover, the number of movies with sequels has significantly increased over time, reflecting a perhaps growing popularity of franchise movies. These type of movies take an increasingly greater fraction of the overall releases.

Financial Performance

Our approach centers on plotting the evolution of earnings from original movies to their sequels. This visual analysis aims to explore the trends and differences in revenue growth or decline between the original films and their subsequent sequels. The analysis uncovers intriguing patterns. Surprisingly, some sequels surpassed their original films, challenging the conventional belief that the first installment often outperforms its successors, and suggesting that certain sequels manage to captivate audiences and generate significant revenue. The scatter plot reveals a positive correlation between the earnings of original movies and their sequels, indicating that sequels often emulate the financial performance of their predecessors.

Genre Dynamics

Moving on to the intrinsic characteristics of movies, we analyzed the genre composition of movies with and without sequels. Our dataset had a large number of subgenres, which made it difficult to compare movies across different genres. Therefore, we simplified our genre analysis by selecting the top 10 most frequent genres in our dataset, and assigning each movie two main genres based on its subgenres. The heatmap allows us to easily visualize the frequency of genre combinations, and we found that movies with sequels are more likely to belong to the action, adventure, comedy, and fantasy genres, while movies without sequels are more likely to belong to the drama, romance, thriller, and horror genres. This suggests that movies with high entertainment value and spectacle are more prone to getting sequels, whereas original films are more suitable for genres that have high emotional appeal and realism. We also performed a statistical test, which confirms that the genre distribution of movies with and without sequels is significantly different.
